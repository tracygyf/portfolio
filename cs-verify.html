Catching bad data before it causes a failed payment

Integrating Nium Verify into a business payments portal so finance teams catch bad recipient data before it causes a failed payment.

Role
Product Designer
Platform
Business Portal
Duration
2 weeks
Problem

Transaction failures and delays

Failed transactions can create a chain reaction of manual follow-up, re-submission through approval workflows, and delayed payroll or supplier payments for clients using our portal.

The data showed a clear pattern: a good chunk of failed transactions traced back to recipients saved with incorrect details.

Our product already runs on the Nium platform, so using Nium Verify to validate recipient details upfront was the logical next step. The brief was simple: integrate it. What that actually meant in practice was left for me to figure out.

⏱

Late timings

Errors only surfaced at payment time, after the full approval chain was done and funds were queued to move.

👁

No signal in the contacts list

Every saved recipient looked identical. There was no way to tell who was verified and who wasn't.

Process

Working out the approach

01

Mapping the problem

The first thing I mapped out was timing. Validation at the point of saving a recipient made the most sense. It's the moment when fixing a mistake costs nothing. Catching it at payment time, after approvals are done, is too late.

I went through the existing flow of adding a recipient, identified every affected touchpoint, and scoped what the integration would actually touch.

02

Designing and hitting a constraint

My first draft kept the verification result inline on the same page as the form. It seemed like the most seamless approach, but the form was built across two steps: recipient details on one, bank details on another. There was no clean place to surface the result without it feeling disconnected from the relevant step.

It got more complicated when we found out Nium Verify could return specific error types. A name mismatch points back to the first step, incorrect bank details to the second. Handling that inline would have meant jumping users back and forth between steps in a way that felt more disorienting than helpful.

Moving the verification result to a dedicated page was the cleaner solution. It gave each error type room to explain itself clearly, with a CTA that redirected users to the right step to fix the specific issue.

03

Reviews and iteration

Shared concepts with the PM and engineering across several rounds. Key feedback shaped the tone of the error copy and the treatment edge cases. Each round tightened the language and the logic behind each state.

Solution

Validate early. Surface the result everywhere it matters.

The integration needed to do two things: catch bad data at the point of saving a recipient, and make sure that information stayed visible everywhere a user might act on it.

The thing that wasn't in the brief

Partway through the project, something occurred to me. Once a user saves a recipient, they land back on the contacts list. But there's nothing there to tell them whether that contact was verified, skipped, or flagged. For any recipient added before this feature existed, there's no signal at all. Users were essentially flying blind every time they picked a contact to pay.

I brought it to the product manager with that reasoning and it got added to scope. The solution was a persistent status badge on each contact in the list.

Most users will verify successfully and move on. But the feature only holds up if every outcome is handled clearly. Early drafts used technical language like "mismatched fields", "verification failed". That tells users what happened, not what to do. I rewrote every status to be clear to users: what to check and how to fix it.

Furthermore, when a user selects any unverified recipient (or any existing recipient prior to the Verify integration) in the send money flow, we surface an inline warning. We don't want to block users from transacting, instead we just give users a clear heads-up that this recipient hasn't been verified and the transaction may be delayed or fail.

Outcome

Shipped, live, and measurable.

The feature is live. Validation now happens at the point of saving a recipient, not at the point of payment. Users have visibility into the health of their contacts list before they ever start a transaction.

20%
reduction in failed transactions from invalid recipient details
Earlier
errors caught at point of creation, before approvals and payment queues
Lower
operational overhead for internal teams chasing manual error follow-up
